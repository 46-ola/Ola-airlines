The Brothers

Neither Wilbur nor Orville Wright finished a university degree. Everything they knew about lift, control, and structure, they taught themselves , first with bicycles, then with kites, then with the sky itself.

1867 — 1912

Wilbur Wright

The elder of the two, Wilbur was the one who first became obsessed with flight after reading about the death of glider pioneer Otto Lilienthal in 1896. He was methodical and quietly stubborn, the one who wrote most of their technical correspondence and who insisted on years of unmanned kite testing before ever trusting a glider with a human pilot. It was Wilbur who worked out that control, not just lift, was the real problem no one else had solved ; an insight that led to wing-warping, the twisting of a wing's tips to bank and steer. He died of typhoid fever in 1912, at just 45, having lived only long enough to see the beginning of the aviation age he started.

1871 — 1948

Orville Wright

Four years younger than Wilbur, Orville was the more instinctive builder of the pair ; sharper with machinery, more willing to tinker until something simply worked. He piloted the Flyer on its first, twelve-second hop over the dunes of Kitty Hawk on December 17, 1903. After Wilbur's death, Orville carried the company and the legacy alone for decades, watching bicycles give way to biplanes and biplanes give way to airliners. He lived to see the jet age begin, dying in 1948, forty-five years almost to the day after the flight that started it all.

From Bicycle Shop to Kitty Hawk

A working timeline of the years that mattered most.

1892
The Wright Cycle Company opens in Dayton

The brothers' bicycle repair and manufacturing shop became their engineering laboratory and their main source of income while they chased flight.

1899 — 1902
Kites, then gliders, at Kitty Hawk

Three seasons of unpowered glider trials on the Outer Banks, chosen for its steady winds and soft sand. By 1902 they had a glider they could reliably control in all three axes.

March 23, 1903
Patent application filed

Before they had even flown under power, the brothers filed for a patent on their flight-control system ; the part of the invention they considered truly original.

December 17, 1903
First powered flight

Orville flew the Wright Flyer for 12 seconds and roughly 120 feet at Kill Devil Hills. Three more flights followed that morning, the longest covering 852 feet in 59 seconds.

1904 — 1905
Huffman Prairie

Back in Ohio, the Flyer II and Flyer III turned a brief hop into sustained, controlled, circling flight : the first truly practical airplane.

May 22, 1906
Patent No. 821,393 granted

After a rejection and the hiring of patent attorney Henry Toulmin, the U.S. Patent Office granted the Wrights' "Flying-Machine" patent ; protecting their method of control, not any one aircraft design.

1908 — 1909
Public demonstrations and the Wright Company

Public flights in France and for the U.S. Army silenced doubters who had never seen the brothers fly. The Wright Company was founded in 1909 to manufacture aircraft commercially.

The Patent

What the Wrights actually protected wasn't a shape : it was an idea about control.

US Patent No. 821,393  ·  Filed March 23, 1903  ·  Granted May 22, 1906

"Flying-Machine" : Orville and Wilbur Wright, of Dayton, Ohio

The patent's real subject is a control system, not a specific airframe: a way of warping a wing's trailing edges to bank the aircraft, combined with a moveable rear rudder to counteract drag and a forward elevator for pitch ; the three-axis control scheme that every fixed-wing aircraft still uses today. It was originally written and filed by the brothers themselves and rejected; only after they hired an Ohio patent attorney in 1904 did the claims hold up. The document that followed ran to seven pages of description, far longer than most patents of its era.
